02What operators actually report

Every example here is a real thread from the last three weeks.

Six patterns cover nearly everything that comes in. Knowing them saves you a lot of time.

1. "This looks wrong" — and about half the time nothing is

This is most of what you'll get. It's also where you can do damage in both directions: file a ticket for something that isn't broken and you burn Engineering's time, wave off something real and you burn the operator's trust. Section 3 is how to tell. The raw material:

2. Data ingestion is what blocks go-lives, not the algorithm

Every activation that stalled in the last three weeks stalled on data. Not once on pricing logic.

  • Best of Switzerland: the Pilatus autumn options are bookable in Ventrata and on GetYourGuide, but nothing is ingesting into Walkway. So Pilatus stays off while Zurich and Grindelwald go live.
  • Boost Portugal: product names and IDs were restructured, several products consolidated into fewer with options. History only reaches back to January and 3–6 months is the minimum. Miguel and Sofia have to agree which history maps to which new ID before anything gets sent.
  • Boost again: the Porto products sit under "Blue Dragon," not "Porto," because of how Ventrata pulls them. Nobody would guess that.
  • Tours for Today: new PAX typologies on Select need a refresh before rules can be set.
Habit worth buildingWhen an operator says "it's live in my system," that's the start of the check, not the end. Live in the reservation system isn't the same as ingesting into Walkway, which isn't the same as mapped, which isn't the same as pushing.

3. Access, permissions and users are a real chunk of the job

Not interesting, but constant. In three weeks: three Boston department heads needed platform access, Key West Ghosts & Gravestones permissions, Michael Long couldn't reset his password, Tim Carr's account was missing the edit option entirely. All of it blocks someone, and all of it is quick once you know where to look.

4. "The price" is genuinely ambiguous, and that causes most of the confusion

HTA Boston runs a 15% advance discount and a 5% same-day discount, plus tax. One product, four defensible prices. The only number that matters to the operator is the pre-tax dollar amount, with the after-tax price second because that's what sells at terminals. Jason's own workaround is the "New Order" view, which shows the clean pushed price without the discount maths on top. That same ambiguity is what caused the 15 July bug.

Useful to know — the OTA "from price"Viator and GetYourGuide build their "from" price off the lowest price in the next 20–30 days. So keep at least one date in that window at base or minimum, otherwise the listing shows an inflated "from" price and loses clicks to cheaper competitors. Critical for hop-on-hop-off, still relevant elsewhere.

5. We can't always scope a push as narrowly as the operator expects

  • Amigo: everything arriving through Ventrata's direct API is tagged with the same source. Ventrata gives us a source-level ID, not a reseller-level one. Go City works on a flat negotiated rate and should never be dynamically priced, but there's no way to filter it out. Escalated, no fix yet.
  • Evan Evans: the test push landed across all live distributor accounts, not just the test account. Go-live is blocked on that, not on our pricing.

03Real fault, or does it just look broken?

Probably the most useful half hour in here.

Work these in order. Most reports resolve before step 5. Every example names the operator, the person and the date, so you can go and read the original.

1

Get the specifics first

Product name and ID, option or unit, the exact date and time slot, a screenshot or short video, and which screen they're on. Without those you can't tell any of the cases below apart.

Jason Renault at HTA is the model. On 15 July he sent a video, then followed up 35 seconds later with "Sorry this is Boston Old Town Trolley – 1-Day, but same is occurring on all products." He scoped it himself. Juan had a fix out within two hours.

2

Is it a displayed price or a pushed price?

Different systems, different failures. Check what we pushed, then what the reservation system holds, then what the real selling channel shows. All three can disagree.

Evan Evans, 20 July. We pushed £164, Prio accepted it, the website showed about £200. Three layers, one broken, and it wasn't ours. The reversion had the same fingerprint — £149.25 instead of £149.

3

Is there a discount or tax layer in between?

If the gap is the same amount or the same percentage every time, it's a layer, not a pricing fault. Ask what discounts are configured and get to the pre-tax number before concluding anything.

HTA on 15 July: we were showing discounted, not base. Evan Evans: £37.25 every single time, across every unit. Consistency is the tell.

4

Did the setting actually save?

Toggles in the algorithm settings don't stick until saved. Before escalating any "I changed it and it reverted," ask them to walk you through the exact clicks.

HTA, 14 July, Boston Old Town Trolley 1-Day. PA answered in nine minutes. Jason's reply: "Doh! That worked!" Eleven minutes end to end.

5

Do two people at the same operator see different numbers?

Then it's an account or permissions problem, not a platform one. Check which base price each account is pulling and what role each user has.

HTA Boston, 22 July, still open. Tim Carr saw a recommendation to move $65→$71. Jason saw a $58 base on the same product. Mackenzie and Michael both matched Jason, so Tim was the only outlier — which is what told us it was his account, not the product. His account was also missing the edit option.

6

Is the number just rounded?

The calendar rounds. Check somewhere with full precision before treating a few cents as a defect.

A cell reading $58 could be $57.51 or $58.49. Harmless by itself, but it makes people doubt every other number on the screen, so name it before they have to.

7

Nothing pushed at all?

Different problem. Check in this order: auto-apply on, active dates in range, credentials valid with write access, mapping intact, and whether the target channel and catalogue accept writes.

Read-only API keys are the sneaky one — they look completely valid and fail silently. And a push can succeed into the wrong catalogue, so it shows as applied and verified on our side while the operator sees nothing change.

Filing it, if it's real

A good ticket lets Engineering start straight away. A weak one hands the discovery work back to them and slows the operator's answer.

  • Title: [Customer] [System] — symptom and business impact
  • Context: user, product/option/unit IDs, reservation system, onboarding phase, auto-apply status
  • Expected vs actual: one clear sentence each. Never "does not work."
  • Timeline: when it started, last known good, timezone, affected dates, still happening?
  • Evidence: screenshots or recording, error text, price values, currency, channel, bounds
  • Impact: live price risk, activation delay, products affected, how the operator is feeling about it
  • Containment: autopilot paused? rule changed? operator informed?
  • Comms: last message sent and the next update you promised

07Nine scenarios

All real. Write your answer before you open the reveal.

For each one: what do you check, in what order, and what exactly do you send. Write the reply out in full.

1. The toggle that won't stick

An operator's revenue manager emails with a video: he was testing the algorithm settings on a hop-on-hop-off product, turned the capacity factor off, and now can't turn it back on.

Reveal

Not a fault. The setting isn't saved until it's saved. Ask him to walk you through the exact clicks before escalating anything.

HTA, 14 July. PA answered in nine minutes and Jason's reply was "Doh! That worked!" Eleven minutes start to finish.

The bit that takes care: telling a competent operator he missed a button is delicate. Put it on the product, not on him — "that save step isn't obvious enough, you're not the first" — and actually log the friction rather than just closing the thread.

2. Two people, two numbers

A depot GM says his calendar shows a recommendation to move $65 → $71 on the 27th. The revenue manager at the same operator, same product, sees a $58 base. Two other users match the revenue manager.

Reveal

An account problem, not a platform one. What tells you is that two of the four are correct. Check which base price the outlier's account is pulling, and his permissions while you're in there.

HTA Boston, 22 July. Tim Carr was the outlier, Mackenzie and Michael both matched Jason. Tim's account also only showed "disable autopilot" with no edit option. Still open.

The bit that takes care: don't tell either of them the other is wrong. "You're seeing different data because your account is pulling a different base price — that's ours to fix, not yours."

3. The consistent gap

You push £164. The partner system confirms it. The operator's website shows about £200. Every product, every unit, always the same £37.25 difference. A reversion lands at £149.25 instead of £149.

Reveal

A consistent gap is a layer, not a pricing fault — and this one sits downstream of us. Don't file it against our pricing engine. Escalate to the partner with the evidence and hold further pushes until they explain it.

Evan Evans through Prio, 20 July. Worth noting 28 August pushed fine while September dates failed, which points at Prio season-mapping. Go-live is blocked on this, not on our pricing.

The bit that takes care: the operator doesn't care whose system is at fault. "We've traced it to the price path between Prio and your site, we're working it with them, I'll update you Thursday" is better than explaining the architecture.

4. The partial fix

An operator reports backoffice prices are wrong across four tour-length options. Engineering fixes it. You tell her it's sorted. She replies: only the two-hour option was corrected, the other three still show different prices depending on the time slot.

Reveal

Never close on your own verification. Ask for specific examples — product, option, date, time slot, what she sees versus what she expects — and re-open with those attached.

Boost Portugal, Sofia Mira, 16–17 July. She checks. She'll always check.

The bit that takes care: a partial fix costs more goodwill than no fix, because it spends the operator's patience twice. Before you say "resolved," check the scope of what was actually fixed against the scope of what was reported.

5. Applied, verified, and nothing happened

A new Bókun activation. Walkway logs the push as applied and verified. The reservation system accepted and confirmed the base-price change. The operator says nothing has changed on his side.

Reveal

Two candidates, and ask about them one at a time. First: do the API keys have edit access, not just read? Read-only keys look completely valid and fail silently. Second: which catalogue is the price landing in? A push can succeed into the wrong catalogue and be invisible where the operator is looking.

The bit that takes care: some contacts answer a specific question and ignore an open-ended one. If you're not getting answers, try asking for exactly one thing.

6. "Now let's raise the ceiling"

After a strong first month on one product, the operator's CEO replies to your report: great results, let's raise the ceiling, we can sell these even higher.

Reveal

That's the believer threshold, and it's the strongest expansion signal you'll get. Someone asking for more headroom unprompted has stopped needing convincing.

Amigo, José Arozarena, 15 July, on Frida Kahlo: "ahora vamos a subir el techo, podemos venderlos más caros todavía."

The bit that takes care: don't just action the ceiling change, that's the customer service answer. Action it and put the next cluster of products in front of him with a date. The moment they're pulling is the moment to ask.

7. Live everywhere except here

An operator confirms a set of seasonal options are bookable in their reservation system and on GetYourGuide. In Walkway they generate nothing, because no price or availability data is coming in.

Reveal

Live in the reservation system doesn't mean ingesting into Walkway. Trigger a refresh, and ask the operator to check whether those options are configured differently on their side — seasonal products often are.

Best of Switzerland, Pilatus autumn options, 21 July. The call we made was to hold Pilatus off entirely and go live with Zurich and Grindelwald, which were clean.

The bit that takes care: don't give a go-live date for the blocked product. Split the activation and say clearly which products are going live now and which are waiting on what. A partial go-live with a clear reason builds more trust than a full date you miss.

8. The catalogue moved under you

Mid-activation, an operator restructures their catalogue — several separate products consolidated into fewer products with options. Historical data only reaches back to January and no longer maps to the new IDs. Go-live is a week out.

Reveal

This is a data problem they have to solve with you, and it needs named owners or it drifts. Someone on their side maps old products to new IDs before anything is sent. Someone else confirms which options are active on which OTA.

Boost Portugal, 21 July. Miguel owns the data file, Sofia owns the mapping and OTA confirmation, August 1 target. Also worth knowing: their Porto products sit under "Blue Dragon," which is exactly the kind of detail that costs a week if it surfaces late.

The bit that takes care: when the blocker is on their side, be explicit about who does what by when. Vagueness reads as patience and behaves as delay.

9. The reseller that shouldn't be priced

An operator tells you one of their resellers works on a flat negotiated rate and must never be dynamically priced — but it is, and it's showing up at reconciliation. They want it excluded.

Reveal

You can't yet, and you should say so plainly. Ventrata gives us a source-level identifier only — everything through the direct API is tagged the same way, with no reseller-level ID to filter on. It's a real limitation we've escalated, not a misconfiguration.

Amigo and Go City, 21 July. Ana raised it and there's no fix on their side either.

The bit that takes care: this is the hardest kind of answer — a real limitation with no workaround and no date. Name it honestly, say what's being done, and don't invent a timeline. "We've asked them for reseller-level IDs, and until that exists we can't filter it. Here's the interim option" beats optimism you can't back up.

08Email templates

Draft four on Friday. Two worked examples to calibrate against.

Rules, before the words

  • Never promise a fix date you don't control. Promise an update time instead, and keep it whether or not you have news.
  • Never guess at a cause on a live pricing issue. "We haven't confirmed the cause yet so I don't want to speculate" is a complete, professional sentence.
  • Open with "How about…" or "I propose we…" rather than "We can get you…"
  • Don't claim uplift the data doesn't support. "We captured X above base pricing in this period" is defensible. "We increased your revenue by X%" often isn't.
  • Match the language. Spanish for Tomás, Amigo and Friends Guides.
  • Respect the CC list. Tom Macklin asked for Prio and his whole team on every thread. Dropping someone is a visible mistake.

Corrects without condescension, puts the shortcoming on the product rather than the person, and closes the loop by actually logging it. The middle paragraph is the one that matters — take it out and it reads as "you did it wrong."

09Reading — and writing — a report

The thing every expansion conversation runs off.

Expansion is a selling motion, not a friendly check-in that mentions more products. It has a business case, a proposal, a decision and a date. The report is what makes that possible, and the same five layers work whether you're reading one or building one.

LayerWhat goes in itRead it for
1. Health firstIs it pushing reliably? Any incidents in the period?Never lead with revenue on an account with an unresolved technical problem. It reads as not paying attention.
2. What the system didPrice movement, range, frequency, behaviour by lead timeEmpire's Chicago review is the model — "prices higher 8–14 days out, holds at the last minute." That's a system you can explain, not a black box.
3. What it producedRevenue captured above base, gross booking value managed, price per guest — and guest volumeVolume is the one people forget. Empire's "+2.1 guests a day" kills the "you're just raising prices and losing customers" objection before it comes up.
4. What's uncertainSeasonality you can't isolate, thin data, a product that needs longerThis is what makes the rest believable. A report with no uncertainty in it reads as marketing.
5. The askA named next cluster, a reason, a dateNot "let us know if you'd like to add more." That's the difference between a report and a proposal.
The three to read on Friday HTA portfolio — $717K captured above flat pricing across 18 actively managed products. Ciao Florence — €527,562 gross booking value managed since activation. Empire Chicago — about $2,000 uplift on a $32K base over six weeks, price per guest up 10% on Secret Interiors and 3% on Gangsters & Ghosts, Memorial Day weekend at $38.30 against $32 flat.
